Most of what I understand, I understood *while writing it down* — not before. A research notebook is less an archive than a thinking tool. A few habits have made mine actually useful.

## Write before you understand

The temptation is to wait until an idea is clean before recording it. Resist it. The messy, half-formed version is the one that captures *why* you were confused, and that's the part you'll want later.

## Date everything, link liberally

Every entry gets a date and links to the entries it builds on. Over months this turns a pile of notes into a graph you can actually navigate.

## Keep a "loose ends" list

End each session by writing down the next open question, even if it's vague. Tomorrow's-you starts from a prompt instead of a blank page.

> The goal isn't a polished document. It's a trail of breadcrumbs honest enough that you can retrace your own reasoning a month from now.

## What not to do

- Don't optimize the tooling before you have the habit.
- Don't rewrite old entries to make past-you look smarter.
- Don't separate "ideas" from "results" — they belong next to each other.
